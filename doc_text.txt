A Locally Executed Agentic AI Framework for Deduplication, Screening, and Structured Data Extraction in Spine Surgery Systematic Reviews: A PRISMA‑trAIce Compliant Evaluation
Abstract: 
Study design: Methods development and validation study. 
Objective: To develop and validate a locally executed agentic AI framework capable of performing deduplication, screening, and structured data extraction in systematic reviews, with transparent reporting following PRISMA‑trAIce guidelines.
Methods: We designed a multi‑agent pipeline composed of specialised agents for deduplication, title/abstract screening, structured data extraction, and verification. All processes were executed locally to ensure data governance and reproducibility. Human‑in‑the‑loop validation was implemented, with outputs compared against dual independent reviewers across three systematic reviews in spine surgery. Performance was assessed using accuracy, inter‑rater agreement (Cohen’s κ), time savings, and clinically critical error rates.
Results: A total of 6,214 records were processed. Deduplication achieved near‑perfect agreement with human reviewers (κ = 0.98). Title and abstract screening of 4,861 records yielded higher concordance than human screening (κ = 0.91) and reduced the volume requiring full‑text review by 83%. Structured data extraction reached substantial agreement with human extraction (κ = 0.87). Across tasks, the agentic AI framework significantly reduced reviewer time requirements by 88.51% (85.8%-91.5%), corresponding to a mean saving of 19.5 hours per review (p<0.001). Clinically critical discrepancies were rare (<1%) and traceable, with no fabricated or hallucinated data introduced.
Conclusions: A locally executed agentic AI framework can deliver accurate, efficient, and secure automation of systematic review tasks. By integrating multiple agents with human oversight, it achieved strong concordance, minimised critical errors, and significantly reduced workload, demonstrating a reproducible pathway for trustworthy evidence synthesis under PRISMA‑trAIce standards.

Keywords: Agentic AI; Systematic review; Meta‑analysis; Deduplication; Evidence synthesis.





Introduction
Systematic reviews and meta‑analyses occupy a central role in evidence‑based medicine.1 By synthesising findings across multiple studies, they provide the most reliable form of evidence to guide clinical practice, inform policy, and shape future research directions. Unlike single studies, which may be limited by sample size, methodology, or context, systematic reviews integrate diverse data sources to deliver comprehensive and balanced conclusions.2,3 Meta‑analyses further strengthen this process by applying statistical techniques to pool results, quantify effect sizes, and assess heterogeneity.4 Together, these methods underpin clinical guidelines and health technology assessments worldwide.
Despite their importance, systematic reviews and meta‑analyses are notoriously time‑consuming and labour-intensive, resulting in reduced methodological rigour.5,6 The process typically begins with exhaustive literature searches across multiple databases, yielding thousands of records. Deduplication must then be performed to remove overlapping citations, a task complicated by inconsistent bibliographic formats and incomplete metadata. Title and abstract screening requires careful application of inclusion and exclusion criteria, often demanding multiple rounds of human review to ensure consistency. Full‑text screening adds another layer of complexity, as reviewers must interpret nuanced methodological details and clinical contexts. Finally, structured data extraction demands meticulous conversion of reported outcomes into analyzable formats, a stage prone to transcription errors and inconsistencies. Collectively, these tasks can take months or even years to complete, delaying the translation of evidence into practice.7
The exponential growth of scientific literature has intensified these challenges.8 Biomedical databases now index millions of articles, with thousands added weekly. This sheer volume threatens the feasibility of traditional review methods, as human reviewers struggle to keep pace. In parallel, the demand for timely evidence has increased, particularly in rapidly evolving fields such as oncology, infectious diseases, and digital health. The tension between the need for speed and the requirement for rigour has created a pressing methodological bottleneck.9,10
The era of artificial intelligence (AI) is reshaping this landscape.11 Advances in machine learning, natural language processing, and large language models have demonstrated the potential to automate discrete tasks within evidence synthesis.12 AI systems can rapidly identify duplicates, classify abstracts, and extract structured data, offering efficiency gains that were previously unattainable.13 However, most current implementations remain limited. They are often single‑task tools, designed to perform one function in isolation, without integration across the multi‑stage workflow of systematic reviews. Moreover, many operate as opaque cloud‑based services, raising concerns about transparency, reproducibility, and data governance.14
Agentic AI represents a new paradigm.15 Unlike monolithic models, agentic AI systems are composed of multiple specialised agents, each designed to perform a specific task while coordinating with others through autonomous reasoning, memory retention, and iterative task decomposition.15,16 This modular architecture mirrors the workflow of systematic reviews, making agentic AI particularly well‑suited to evidence synthesis. By embedding human‑in‑the‑loop validation, agentic AI frameworks can combine efficiency with oversight, ensuring that critical decisions remain traceable and clinically reliable.
The objective of this study is to develop and validate a locally executed agentic AI framework capable of supporting systematic reviews and meta‑analyses by automating key tasks—deduplication, title and abstract screening, and structured data extraction—while retaining human oversight for full‑text screening. 
Methods
Study Design
This study was conducted as a methods development and validation project to evaluate a locally executed agentic AI framework for systematic reviews and meta‑analyses. The study was reported in adherence to PRISMA‑trAIce (PRISMA–Transparent Reporting of Artificial Intelligence in Comprehensive Evidence Synthesis) guidelines to ensure transparency, reproducibility, and methodological rigour.17 PRISMA‑trAIce is particularly relevant because it addresses the “black box” nature of AI systems, requiring explicit documentation of inputs, outputs, and validation processes.
Development Phase
The agentic AI pipeline was initially developed and refined using 26 previously published systematic reviews authored by the investigators.18–43 These reviews spanned diverse topics in spine surgery and provided a rich training ground for defining operational logic, extraction schemas, and error‑handling protocols. During this phase, the AI agents were iteratively tuned to replicate the workflows of experienced reviewers. Deduplication rules were derived from bibliographic inconsistencies encountered in these reviews, while screening logic was informed by inclusion/exclusion criteria previously applied. Extraction schemas were standardised to capture binary outcomes (e.g., event counts such as infection present/absent), continuous measures (mean ± standard deviation), and categorical variables (e.g., study design, sample size). Importantly, this development phase was distinct from the validation phase, ensuring that subsequent performance testing was conducted independently.
Validation Phase
Validation was performed using three de novo systematic reviews in spine surgery: 1. Glucagon‑like peptide‑1 analogues in spine surgery; 2. Giant cell tumours in the cervical spine; and 3. Return to work following spinal cord injury. For these reviews, independent human reviewers who had no involvement in the development of the AI system established the “ground truth”. Each record was screened and extracted by two blinded reviewers, with discrepancies resolved by consensus. This design avoided circularity and ensured that AI outputs were compared against an unbiased reference standard.
System Architecture
The framework was implemented as a multi‑agent pipeline, with each agent responsible for a distinct stage of the review process. First, a deduplication agent identified and removed duplicate records using DOI matching, title similarity, and author/year concordance. Next, a screening agent applied protocol‑defined inclusion and exclusion criteria to titles and abstracts, followed by an extraction agent that captured structured data fields (sample sizes, binary outcomes, continuous measures with standard deviations) using predefined schemas. Finally, a verification agent monitored logs, flagged discrepancies, and directed human adjudication. Agents communicated through a locally executed coordination layer, ensuring that all processes remained under researcher control. Unlike cloud‑based AI services, this design safeguarded data governance and reproducibility.
Data Sources and Preparation
Bibliographic records and full‑text PDFs were retrieved from biomedical databases such as PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and Cochrane Library for three validation reviews. Inclusion and exclusion criteria were established a priori based on the research question. All records were imported into the AI pipeline for processing.
Deduplication
The deduplication agent compared records across multiple fields (DOI, title, author, year). In cases of conflict, where duplication could not be confidently determined, both documents were retained to avoid inadvertently removing valid entries. This conservative approach safeguarded against loss of relevant studies. The deduplication agent execution code is given in the GitHub repository link. Deduplication outputs were logged and cross‑checked against independent reviewers.
Title and Abstract Screening
The screening agent applied inclusion and exclusion criteria to titles and abstracts. A dual-pass strategy was employed to ensure consistency in the AI decisions for screening, where each screening task was executed twice independently. Only when both outputs matched was the decision finalised and exported into Google Sheets (Google LLC, Workspace v2025.2). This dual‑pass strategy minimised random error and reinforced consistency.  We also employed a cross‑validation approach to investigate the accuracy of the screening process. Specifically, the screening agent’s outputs were randomly partitioned into two subsets. Separate human reviewers independently adjudicated each subset, and discrepancies were reconciled. This design ensured that AI decisions were validated against independent ground truth rather than self‑consistency. Screening outputs were exported into spreadsheets for verification. The screening agent execution code is given in the GitHub repository link.
Full‑Text Screening
Full‑text screening was conducted exclusively by human reviewers. This ensured that nuanced eligibility criteria and methodological details were adjudicated with clinical judgment and rigour. AI agents were not applied at this stage, but the verification agent ensured that adjudication logs were complete and traceable.
Structured Data Extraction
The extraction agent applied a predefined schema to capture key variables. Deterministic rules converted reported percentages into counts and standardised continuous outcomes. To enhance robustness, extraction tasks were subjected to cross‑agent redundancy: two independent extraction agents processed the same dataset using slightly different parsing strategies. Outputs were compared, and discrepancies were flagged for human adjudication. For entries with missing data, justification logs were generated, documenting the rationale for incomplete extraction and flagging these cases for human adjudication. The data extraction agent execution code is given in the GitHub repository link.
Verification and Human Oversight
The verification agent continuously monitored logs, identified discrepancies, and directed them to human reviewers. Dual independent reviewers validated AI outputs for deduplication, screening, and extraction. Discrepancies flagged by the verification agent were resolved through consensus adjudication. This human‑in‑the‑loop design ensured that while the AI framework accelerated routine tasks, critical decisions remained traceable and clinically reliable. Audit trails documented the origin of each data element, the agent responsible, and any human intervention. This design ensured that while the AI framework accelerated routine tasks, critical decisions remained traceable and clinically reliable. Importantly, all records processed by the system were verified for validity, with human reviewers confirming that no fabricated or hallucinated data were introduced. The verification agent execution code is given in the GitHub repository link.
Output and Validation
All outputs were exported into Google Sheets, which served as the central platform for verification and validation. This allowed reviewers to systematically check AI decisions, adjudicate discrepancies, and confirm final datasets.
Performance Measures
Performance in this study was evaluated across four key dimensions to ensure methodological rigour and transparency. Accuracy was assessed as the concordance between AI outputs and human reviewers, providing a direct measure of reliability. Inter‑rater agreement was quantified using Cohen’s κ, offering a standardised metric of consistency across decisions. Time savings were calculated by comparing the reduction in human effort achieved through AI‑assisted workflows against traditional manual processes, with statistical testing applied to confirm significance. Finally, error classification was performed by categorising discrepancies as either minor, which did not affect clinical interpretation, or clinically critical, which had the potential to alter conclusions. This multidimensional evaluation framework allowed for a comprehensive assessment of the agentic AI system’s performance in systematic review tasks.
Statistical Analysis
All statistical analyses were performed using Stata (StataCorp LLC, version 17.0, College Station, TX, USA). Descriptive statistics were used to summarise record counts, workflow outputs, and reviewer workload. Accuracy was calculated as the proportion of concordant decisions between AI agents and human reviewers. Inter‑rater agreement was quantified using Cohen’s κ, interpreted according to Landis and Koch benchmarks (κ < 0.20 = slight, 0.21–0.40 = fair, 0.41–0.60 = moderate, 0.61–0.80 = substantial, >0.80 = almost perfect agreement). Time efficiency was assessed by comparing the mean time required for AI‑assisted workflows against traditional human‑only workflows. Paired comparisons were conducted using a paired t‑test. Error classification was performed by categorising discrepancies into minor (non‑impactful to clinical interpretation) and clinically critical (potential to alter conclusions). All statistical tests were two‑sided, with significance set at p < 0.05. 
Hardware and Software
The pipeline was executed locally using a Gemini agent (Google DeepMind, release 2025.1)44 with temperature set to 0.2 and max tokens capped at 2,048. Antigravity software (Google, v3.4) coordinated agent communication. The agentic AI pipeline was executed locally on a standard consumer laptop rather than a high‑performance workstation. The configuration included 16 GB of RAM, an integrated graphics processor with 8 GB capacity, and a solid‑state drive for storage. The system operated on a stable 5G network connection, which ensured rapid communication between agents and efficient handling of large bibliographic datasets. Reporting these specifications ensures reproducibility and provides context for computational costs.
Results 
Dataset and Workflow
Across the three systematic reviews included in this validation study, a total of 6,214 bibliographic records were retrieved from PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and the Cochrane Library. After import into the agentic AI pipeline, records were processed sequentially through deduplication, title and abstract screening, full‑text screening, and structured data extraction. All outputs were exported into Google Sheets for verification and adjudication. Importantly, the validation was performed by independent reviewers not involved in system development, ensuring that accuracy metrics were unbiased and free from circularity.
Deduplication Performance
The deduplication agent identified 1,032 duplicate records using DOI matching, title similarity, and author/year concordance. Validation against human reviewers demonstrated accuracy of 98.7%, with Cohen’s κ = 0.96 (0.94–0.98), indicating almost perfect agreement. This performance was comparable to established reference management tools. In cases of uncertainty, duplicates were retained to avoid inadvertent exclusion, and justification logs documented the rationale. The detailed inter-rater reliability between the human and AI agent is given in Table 1. 
Title and Abstract Screening
A total of 5,182 records were subjected to title and abstract screening. While reviewers identified and excluded 4861 duplicate records, the AI agent removed 98.5% of the reviewer-identified duplicate records. The dual‑pass strategy yielded κ = 0.91 (0.89–0.93) compared with human reviewers, reflecting strong concordance. The AI pipeline reduced the number of records requiring full‑text review by 82%, substantially decreasing reviewer workload as shown in Table 1. The screening robustness was enhanced through cross‑validation and stratified sampling. Outputs were partitioned by publication year, then independently adjudicated by separate reviewers. This ensured that accuracy was validated against human ground truth. 
Full‑Text Screening
Full‑text screening was conducted exclusively by human reviewers. Of the 329 records that progressed to this stage, 72 studies met the inclusion criteria. AI agents were not applied here, but the verification agent ensured that all adjudication logs were complete and traceable.
Structured Data Extraction
The extraction agent processed the 72 included studies, capturing structured data fields such as sample sizes, binary outcomes, and continuous measures with standard deviations that amounted to 1550 total data fields. Dual‑pass extraction yielded κ = 0.87 (0.86–0.88) compared with human reviewers with 93% concordance, representing substantial agreement. Justification logs were generated for 6.7% of entries with missing or ambiguous data, all of which were directed to human adjudication. Absent standard deviations, merged table cells, or incomplete subgroup reporting most commonly triggered missing data logs (6.7%). For example, several studies reported mean values without variance measures, requiring human adjudication. 
Verification and Human Oversight
The verification agent flagged 104 discrepancies across deduplication, screening, and extraction tasks. All were reviewed by dual independent human adjudicators, with consensus reached in every case. Audit trails confirmed that no fabricated data were introduced; every extracted element was traceable to its source document. Of the 104 flagged discrepancies from the data extraction, 15 were found to be critical (0.9% of the total data fields extracted). Examples include swapped cohorts (e.g., intervention vs. control mislabeling) and misinterpretation of composite outcomes. These were consistently flagged by the verification agent and corrected by human reviewers. 
Time Efficiency
The agentic AI framework significantly reduced reviewer time requirements by 88.51% (85.8%-91.5%), corresponding to a mean saving of 19.5 hours per review (p<0.001), as shown in Table 1. Time efficiency was most pronounced in the data extraction phase.
Error Classification
Discrepancies were categorised as minor or clinically critical. Minor discrepancies accounted for 86% of flagged outputs, typically involving formatting inconsistencies, ambiguous abbreviations, or incomplete metadata. Clinically critical errors (14%) were rare, 0.9% of total outputs, typically involving mislabelling cohorts, incorrect denominators, or omission of adverse events. For example, misclassification of “revision surgery” as a primary outcome rather than a secondary endpoint could have altered the interpretation. These examples highlight the necessity of the human‑in‑the‑loop design and were consistently identified by the verification agent and resolved through human adjudication. 
Discussion
The present study demonstrates the feasibility and utility of a locally executed agentic AI framework for systematic reviews and meta‑analyses. By integrating multiple specialised agents—deduplication, screening, extraction, and verification—into a coordinated pipeline, the framework achieved high accuracy, strong inter‑rater agreement, substantial time savings, and minimal clinically critical errors. Importantly, the system was designed to operate entirely on local devices, thereby safeguarding personal data and ensuring reproducibility. Further, validation was performed against independent reviewers not involved in system development, thereby avoiding circularity and ensuring that accuracy metrics reflected unbiased ground truth. The key outcomes of this study highlight that agentic AI can substantially reduce the workload associated with evidence synthesis while maintaining methodological rigour and transparency.  
One of the most significant contributions of this work is the demonstration that multi‑agent coordination can replicate and enhance the traditional workflow of systematic reviews. Deduplication was performed with near‑perfect accuracy, screening achieved strong concordance with human reviewers, and structured data extraction yielded substantial agreement. The verification agent ensured that discrepancies were systematically identified and adjudicated during every stage of the review process, preventing the propagation of errors. Together, these outcomes illustrate that agentic AI can serve as a reliable partner in evidence synthesis, reducing repetitive manual tasks while preserving the integrity of clinical decision‑making.  
When considering the broader landscape, it is essential to acknowledge that independent software solutions already exist for each of the discrete tasks performed in this pipeline. For example, EndNote (Clarivate Analytics)45 and Zotero (Corporation for Digital Scholarship)46 are widely used for reference management and deduplication. Screening tasks can be performed using platforms such as Covidence (Veritas Health Innovation)47 or Rayyan (Qatar Computing Research Institute)48, which provide interfaces for title and abstract screening. Structured data extraction is supported by tools like DistillerSR (Evidence Partners)49, which offers customizable forms and workflows. Verification and adjudication can be managed through collaborative platforms such as EPPI‑Reviewer (UCL Institute of Education)50. While these tools are robust and widely adopted, they are typically subscription‑based, requiring institutional or personal licenses.  
The reliance on paid software introduces barriers to accessibility, particularly for researchers in resource‑limited settings.51 Subscription costs can be prohibitive, and cloud‑based platforms often raise concerns about data privacy and governance.52 In contrast, the current framework offers a free, locally executable solution that does not compromise personal security. By operating entirely on local devices, the system ensures that sensitive bibliographic records and extracted data remain under the researcher’s control. This design choice addresses a critical gap in the current ecosystem, providing an accessible and secure alternative to paid platforms.  
Each of the key takeaways from this study carries broader implications. First, the demonstration of high accuracy and inter‑rater agreement underscores the potential of agentic AI to standardise evidence synthesis. In traditional workflows, variability between reviewers can introduce bias; by contrast, AI agents apply criteria consistently, reducing subjectivity. Second, the substantial time savings highlight the efficiency gains achievable through automation. Systematic reviews are often delayed by the sheer volume of records requiring screening; by reducing this burden, agentic AI accelerates the production of timely evidence. Third, the emphasis on error classification and verification illustrates the importance of transparency. By categorising discrepancies and directing them to human adjudication, the system ensures that critical errors are not overlooked. Unlike subscription‑based cloud platforms, this framework was executed locally on a consumer‑grade laptop. This demonstrates that advanced AI‑assisted workflows can be implemented without specialised infrastructure, thereby lowering barriers to adoption. Nevertheless, computational costs and hardware requirements should be acknowledged. While feasible on mid‑range consumer devices, large‑scale reviews may benefit from more powerful systems to optimise processing speed. Finally, the local execution of the framework demonstrates that efficiency need not come at the expense of security. In an era of increasing concern about data privacy, this design provides reassurance that sensitive information remains protected.  
Despite these strengths, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the framework was validated across a limited number of systematic reviews in spine surgery, and further testing across diverse domains is necessary to confirm generalizability. Second, while the dual‑pass strategy and cross-validation approach for screening and extraction enhance reliability, it also increases computational demands, which may limit scalability for extensive datasets. Third, full‑text screening was intentionally retained as a human‑only task, reflecting the complexity of adjudicating nuanced eligibility criteria. While this preserves methodological rigour, it also means that the framework does not yet achieve complete automation. Fourth, although the system is free and locally executable, technical expertise is required to implement and maintain the pipeline, which may pose challenges for researchers without computational backgrounds. Finally, while the integration of Antigravity software and Gemini agent enhanced performance, reliance on specific versions of proprietary software may limit reproducibility if future updates alter functionality.  
Conclusion 
This study provides evidence that a locally executed agentic AI framework can substantially reduce the workload of systematic reviews and meta‑analyses while maintaining transparency, reproducibility, and clinical reliability. By integrating multiple specialised agents into a coordinated pipeline, the system achieved high accuracy, strong inter‑rater agreement, significant time savings, and minimal clinically critical errors. Unlike existing independent software solutions, which are subscription‑based and cloud‑dependent, this framework is free to execute locally, ensuring accessibility and safeguarding personal security. While limitations remain, particularly regarding scalability and the need for human oversight in full‑text screening, the findings demonstrate a promising pathway for integrating agentic AI into evidence synthesis. Future work should focus on expanding validation across diverse domains, refining automation of complex tasks, and enhancing usability for non‑technical researchers. Ultimately, this framework represents a step toward democratising systematic reviews, enabling timely and trustworthy evidence synthesis in an era of rapidly expanding biomedical literature.
